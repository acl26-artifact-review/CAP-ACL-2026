S:
The patient reports left shoulder pain for the last three weeks, with no recall of injury or trauma. He reports the pain is constant and worsens with activity, particularly reaching and overhead movements. He reports the pain does not radiate and is primarily located at the shoulder. He has not taken any medication for the pain and has iced it initially but not recently. He denies numbness or tingling in the arm or fingers. He reports a normal capillary refill.

O:
Physical exam reveals limited active and passive range of motion in the left shoulder. Palpation of the greater tuberosity reveals tenderness. Hand grip strength is normal. Neurologic exam shows intact light touch sensation and capillary refill is less than three seconds. A prior x-ray of the left shoulder revealed no fractures or bony abnormalities.

A:
The patient's symptoms are most likely due to rotator cuff tendinopathy. There is no evidence of fracture or other structural abnormality on the prior x-ray.

P:
Order an MRI of the left shoulder to evaluate for rotator cuff pathology. Refer the patient to physical therapy for a course of approximately six to eight weeks to strengthen the shoulder musculature. Continue with Tylenol as needed for pain relief. Follow up in four weeks to assess response to physical therapy and MRI results.
