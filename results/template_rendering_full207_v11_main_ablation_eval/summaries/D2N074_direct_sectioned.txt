Chief Complaint
Worsening headaches over the past few months.

History of Present Illness
The patient reports worsening headaches over the past few months, initially rated 3/10 in severity and gradually increasing to 6/10. The headaches are located behind the eyes and described as a dull, nagging ache, lasting several hours. They are worse in the morning. He denies any prior headaches, fever, rash, neck stiffness, numbness, tingling, or passing out. He has tried acetaminophen with limited relief. He recently noticed difficulty seeing out of the side, which he attributed to bumping into door frames.

Findings
Examination reveals alert and oriented patient with appropriate affect and fluent speech. Cranial nerve examination is grossly intact. Visual acuity and eye movements are normal. Pupils are equal and reactive. Visual field testing reveals bitemporal hemianopia. Color vision is normal. MRI of the brain with contrast demonstrates a pituitary lesion with elevation and compression of the optic chiasm. The ventricles are normal in size.

Assessment
Pituitary adenoma, incidentally discovered during workup for worsening headaches, with clinical and radiographic evidence of optic chiasmal compression.

Plan
Referral to an ophthalmologist for a comprehensive visual field evaluation prior to surgery. Scheduling with the surgical scheduler, Deborah, to arrange for transsphenoidal surgery. Counsel the patient regarding the risks of anesthesia, including but not limited to heart attack, stroke, and death, as well as surgical risks such as infection, need for further surgery, wound complications, and potential neurological sequelae. Follow-up will be arranged after surgery to monitor visual function.
