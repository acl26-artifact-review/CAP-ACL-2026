S:
The patient reports left shoulder pain that began approximately three weeks ago, possibly related to basement renovation work. He describes the pain as constant, exacerbated by pressure and when reaching overhead. He reports difficulty reaching for objects and has been taking two extra strength Tylenol every six to eight hours to manage the pain. He denies numbness or tingling in his arms or hands.

O:
Physical exam reveals limited active and passive range of motion in the left shoulder, with tenderness to palpation over the greater tuberosity. Capillary refill is less than three seconds. Neurological exam is intact, with no sensory deficits. The cervical x-ray shows no fractures.

A:
The patient's left shoulder pain is most likely due to a rotator cuff tendinopathy. The physical exam findings support this diagnosis.

P:
I am ordering a cervical MRI to further evaluate the left shoulder. I am also referring the patient to physical therapy for approximately six to eight weeks. If symptoms do not improve, I will consider a steroid injection. The patient should continue taking Tylenol as needed.
