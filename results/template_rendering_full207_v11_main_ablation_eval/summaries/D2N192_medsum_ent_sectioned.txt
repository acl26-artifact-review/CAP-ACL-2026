Chief Complaint
Abdominal pain

History of Present Illness
Karen is a 34-year-old female presenting with a 10-day history of abdominal pain, initially mild and localized to the right side, now worsening over the past couple of days. She reports the pain is exacerbated by meals and relieved by rest. She describes some nausea and mild heartburn but denies vomiting. Her migraines have been infrequent recently, managed with over-the-counter analgesics and occasional sumatriptan. She reports feeling fatigued. She denies recent travel or unusual exposures. She has a history of hypertension, currently managed with lisinopril 20mg daily, and reports checking her blood pressure approximately three times per week, with readings typically in the 120s-130s over 70s. She has been taking excedrin for migraines as needed and sumatriptan when needed.

Findings
Physical exam reveals tenderness in the right upper quadrant, with an equivocal Murphy's sign. The remainder of the abdominal exam is unremarkable. Cardiovascular exam is normal, with a grade 3/6 systolic ejection murmur. Blood pressure is 132/82 mmHg. No fever detected.

Assessment
The patient presents with acute abdominal pain, likely secondary to right upper quadrant tenderness, raising concern for possible gallbladder pathology or other inflammatory process. Her history of chronic migraines and hypertension is also relevant.

Plan
I am ordering a CBC, comprehensive metabolic panel, urinalysis, and a pregnancy test. I am also ordering a right upper quadrant ultrasound. I will prescribe a refill of lisinopril 20mg and sumatriptan as needed. I will also provide instructions for continued use of excedrin for migraine relief. I will follow up with the patient in four weeks, or sooner if symptoms worsen or if the ultrasound reveals significant abnormalities. I will also schedule a physical therapy consultation to address the right upper quadrant tenderness.
