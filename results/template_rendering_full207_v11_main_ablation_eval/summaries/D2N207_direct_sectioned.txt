Chief Complaint
Shortness of breath.

History of Present Illness
The patient reports a week-long history of shortness of breath, exacerbated by walking and stairs. He previously experienced similar symptoms six months ago, which resolved with lasix dose adjustments. He has a history of coronary artery disease with a stent placed in the right coronary artery and a prior ejection fraction of 35%. He reports a recent increase in salt intake due to holiday gatherings. He denies swelling in his legs. He is currently taking 40 mg of lasix daily. He reports urinating approximately the same amount recently. He reports waking up once or twice at night short of breath. He has been following his lasix dose and has been watching his salt intake. He has been taking tylenol for foot pain over the past week. He has not been told about a history of gout. He has not been told about a history of gout.

Findings
Blood pressure is 130/80 mmHg. Jugular venous distention is 9 cm. Bibasilar crackles are present. A three out of six systolic ejection murmur is heard at the left base. Bilateral pitting edema is present, 1+ to 2+ in the lower extremities.

Assessment
Acute heart failure exacerbation secondary to coronary artery disease and chronic kidney disease stage 3. Possible gout flare.

Plan
Increase lasix dosage to 80 mg daily, divided into two doses, with the morning dose. Order a chest x-ray and echocardiogram. Schedule a follow-up visit in two days to assess response to increased diuretic therapy. Refer a dietitian for dietary counseling. Order a complete metabolic panel. Order uric acid. Consider alternative medications for gout if tylenol is insufficient. Continue to monitor potassium levels and educate the patient on high-potassium foods.
