Chief Complaint
Nonhealing right foot ulcer.

History of Present Illness
The patient reports the right foot ulcer began approximately six weeks ago following the wearing of tight shoes. He initially noticed burning and stinging, which he attributed to a blister. The blister subsequently became unroofed, revealing thick, mushy, foul-smelling tissue. His wife and dog noted a persistent odor. The patient is currently on a ten-day course of antibiotics, having received them for six days. He experienced a temperature of 99.7°F and chills, and now reports throbbing pain in the foot, described as a 'fire poker,' along with cramping in his calf muscle and a red streak ascending his calf. He has also experienced increased coughing and shortness of breath with exertion. His A1c is 6.7%, and he previously had an A1c of 13 while on an insulin pump. He reports trending blood sugars in the 3-400 range over the past two weeks. The patient hiked at Charlie's Bunion approximately one week prior to the ulcer's development, wearing new diabetic shoes and inserts, which he changes every three to four months. He has been regularly hiking with his wife on new trails.

Findings
Physical examination reveals a necrotic wound measuring 2x2 centimeters on the right heel, with sloughing tissue and surrounding cellulitis. Erythema is present, and no bony exposure is noted. Bilateral palpable pulses are present, including femoral and popliteal pulses. Doppler examination confirms the presence of dorsalis pedis and posterior tibial pulses. A slight fever is noted. The patient is coughing and exhibits difficulty with catching his breath.

Assessment
The patient presents with a nonhealing diabetic foot ulcer secondary to diabetic neuropathy, complicated by cellulitis. He is at risk for further complications, including infection and potential need for advanced wound care.

Plan
I will order a blood supply test for the wound and perform a debridement today. A culture will be obtained, and antibiotic therapy will be adjusted based on the culture results. I am concerned about the redness moving up the leg and the calf swelling and pain, and will monitor this closely. I will continue the patient's current antibiotic therapy and consider extending or changing it based on culture results. Follow-up will be scheduled in seven days, and the patient will be referred to his endocrinologist for continued diabetes management.
