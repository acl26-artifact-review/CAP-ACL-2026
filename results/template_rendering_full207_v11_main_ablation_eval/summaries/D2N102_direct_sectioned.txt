Chief Complaint
Evaluation for hematuria and follow-up of previously identified kidney stone.

History of Present Illness
The patient reports experiencing episodes of bright red urine, resembling cherry kool-aid, for the past six weeks, following the passage of two small stones last week. He also reported constant urination and severe pain last week. He rates the pain at the time as an eight on a scale of zero to ten. He reports drinking primarily Mountain Dew and admits to inadequate water intake, estimating approximately 125 ounces daily based on his 250-pound weight. He also consumes foods high in sodium, oxalate, and animal protein. He is taking Alavert 10 milligrams daily and has no known drug allergies.

Findings
The patient's urine dipstick revealed no leukocytes, nitrates, protein, ketone, or bilirubin. The urine color was yellow and clear, with urobilinogen at 0.2 and a pH of 5.5, and a specific gravity of 1.020. A trace amount of blood was observed. Renal ultrasound revealed a right kidney measuring 10 cm by 5 cm, with no stones, masses, or hydronephrosis. The left kidney measures 10.8 cm by 5 cm and contains a 6 mm non-obstructing stone. Pre-void volume was 533 mL and post-void volume was 0 mL. Physical exam revealed no flank pain or tenderness. The abdominal exam was unremarkable. A normal vaginal exam was performed.

Assessment
Chronic urolithiasis with hematuria. No acute obstruction or pain.

Plan
I do not recommend surgical intervention at this time given the absence of obstruction or pain. I recommend drinking approximately 125 ounces of water daily to aid in stone passage and avoiding sugary drinks like Mountain Dew. I recommend limiting sodium intake to less than 1000 mg per day and reducing consumption of beef, poultry, fish, and pork. I have provided a handout detailing dietary recommendations and foods to avoid. Follow up in one year unless there are recurrent episodes of bleeding or pain, at which point you can contact the office sooner. If you experience severe pain or bright red blood in your urine, please go to the emergency room.
