Chief Complaint
Shortness of breath and blood-tinged sputum.

History of Present Illness
Dennis, a 57-year-old male, presents with a two-week history of increasing shortness of breath. He reports that the shortness of breath is exacerbated by activity and occurs frequently, leading to significant distress. He describes waking up with shortness of breath and feeling short of breath upon returning home after any exertion. He reports coughing up blood, initially a few tablespoons per day, now approximately three to four cups daily. He states that the bleeding occurs primarily when he coughs. He has had this problem for approximately three weeks. He reports a history of diabetes, hypertension, and asthma. He notes that his blood sugar has been running high, with readings often over 200 post-meal and around 150 in the morning. He has been wearing a Dexcom and observes spikes after meals. He has been taking metformin, but his blood pressure has been somewhat low, so he occasionally skips his norvasc. He had a knee problem some time ago, but denies current swelling. He reports no bruising or gum bleeding. He denies leg swelling or calf tenderness. He reports no fever. He has been to the office but was unable to get an appointment due to high volume.

Findings
Oxygen saturation is 94% on pulse oximetry. Pulse is regular at 78 beats per minute. Lung exam reveals bilateral rhonchi and scattered trails, with diminished breath sounds on the right. No wheezing is present. Blood pressure is 120/70 mmHg. Heart exam reveals a two+ ejection murmur. No carotid bruits are noted. The abdomen is soft. Lower extremity exam reveals one plus edema in the bilateral lower extremities, with no calf tenderness. Neurological exam is unremarkable.

Assessment
The patient presents with shortness of breath, hemoptysis, and a history of diabetes, hypertension, and asthma. He is likely experiencing a pulmonary process, possibly pneumonia or exacerbation of underlying lung disease. His blood sugar control is suboptimal.

Plan
I am ordering a chest x-ray and a cat scan to evaluate for pneumonia or other pulmonary pathology. I will refer him to the emergency department for further evaluation and management. I will increase his metformin dose from 500mg twice daily to 750mg twice daily. I will also continue his norvasc until we can reassess his blood pressure. I will schedule a follow-up appointment in three weeks to discuss his diabetes management and assess his response to treatment. I will contact the emergency department to coordinate his care.
