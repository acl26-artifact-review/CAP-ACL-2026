S:
The patient reports experiencing right foot pain following a tennis injury, specifically a fall while volleying the ball. He states he wrapped the foot and iced it last night. He denies numbness or tingling. He reports no prior injuries to this foot. The patient is currently playing tennis with friends as a hobby and is trying to learn, but cannot afford lessons. He reports that he is right-handed.

O:
Vitals are stable: blood pressure 120/75, respiratory rate 18, temperature 97.1°F, pulse 60 bpm. Capillary refill is less than 3 seconds in the right foot. Dorsalis pedis pulse is strong. Right foot exam reveals bruising of the plantar and dorsal aspects, associated swelling, tenderness to palpation at the midfoot, and a positive piano key test at the first and second metatarsals. The right foot is warm to the touch. Neurovascular exam reveals intact sensation to light touch and strong pedal pulses. X-ray findings show subtle dorsal displacement of the base of the second metatarsal with a 3 mm separation of the first and second metatarsal bases and a bony fragment in the listerfranc joint space.

A:
The patient presents with a lisfranc fracture of the right foot. The injury is consistent with the mechanism of injury and radiographic findings. Surgery is recommended to stabilize the fracture and prevent long-term complications.

P:
I am recommending surgical intervention for the right foot fracture. An MRI will be ordered to further assess the fracture and any ligament injuries. The patient will be discharged the same day following the procedure and will follow up in approximately one week. A cast and crutches will be provided. Physical therapy will be recommended post-operatively to strengthen muscles and optimize function. The patient will be advised to avoid driving for six to eight weeks post-surgery. Documentation of risks associated with surgery, including bleeding, nerve damage, and loss of sensation, will be provided for the patient's review.
