The patient reports a six-month history of knee pain, exacerbated by squatting and rising from a seated position. He describes the pain as a deep, aching sensation behind his kneecaps and reports occasional crepitus. He recalls no specific injury triggering the initial pain. He reports tenderness to palpation of the bilateral retinacular retinaculum and a positive patellar grind test. He denies swelling or redness in his knees.

O:
Vital signs are normal. Physical exam reveals normal gait, strength 3/5 for abduction bilaterally, and no overlying erythema or effusion in the knees. Lachman tests and McMurray tests are negative bilaterally. X-rays of both knees show no fractures or osteoarthritis.

A:
The patient's symptoms are consistent with patellofemoral pain syndrome, likely related to biomechanical factors and activity level. The MRI supports this diagnosis.

P:
I recommend physical therapy to address lower extremity strength, mobility, and running mechanics. The patient should continue taking ibuprofen or aleve as needed for pain relief. We will continue to assess progress with each visit.
