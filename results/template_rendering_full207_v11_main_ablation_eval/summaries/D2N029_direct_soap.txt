S:
The patient reports intermittent tobacco use, starting in adolescence and finding it difficult to quit. She reports no personal history of skin cancers, but her mother and sister both had melanoma. Her primary care physician examines her moles annually and has not identified any concerning lesions prior to this presentation. The patient denies any unintentional weight changes, headaches, fatigue, nausea, vomiting, or vision changes.

O:
A shave biopsy site measuring 1 centimeter is noted on the right inferior back. There are no other suspicious skin lesions identified on physical exam. A soft lymph node is palpated in the right groin, which is nontender. The ultrasound will be ordered to evaluate the right inguinal lymph node.

A:
The patient has been diagnosed with invasive melanoma, stage 1. A suspicious inguinal lymph node was identified, warranting further investigation with ultrasound. The biopsy revealed a superficial spreading melanoma with a Breslow thickness of 0.7 millimeters.

P:
Arrange for an ultrasound of the right inguinal lymph node. Recommend a wide local excision with a 1-2 centimeter margin, if the ultrasound is negative. Schedule follow-up with the dermatologist for continued monitoring. Advise the patient to apply sunscreen daily and perform regular skin self-exams. Offer to discuss the plan further and address any questions.
