S:
The patient reports experiencing bilateral knee pain for two weeks following a skiing injury. He states the pain is worse with prolonged walking, particularly during walks with his dog, and occasionally reaches a severity of eleven on a pain scale. He reports a pop sensation in his left knee at the time of injury. The patient denies any other hospitalizations or surgeries in the past, except for a previous reconstruction of his right knee at age fourteen due to a gymnastics injury. He describes a history of being a 'tough' athlete with a high pain tolerance. He currently consumes THC gummies from his grandmother. He has noticed mild swelling and bruising in both knees.

O:
Physical exam reveals mild swelling and bruising in both knees. Gait reveals a slight limp. Range of motion is generally intact, although the patient reports some stiffness in the morning. Dorsiflexion causes pain, while plantar flexion does not. The patient's knee bends fully, and there is no observable instability.

A:
The patient's presentation is consistent with a posterior cruciate ligament strain in the right knee, likely exacerbated by previous injury and contributing to the observed limp. The left knee appears to be unstable and may be related to the prior reconstruction.

P:
I recommend continuing to brace the right knee to reduce stress. I recommend physical therapy three times per week to improve strength and stability. I recommend avoiding skiing and gymnastics activities for now. I prescribe meloxicam 15 mg daily for right knee pain and ibuprofen 800 mg twice daily for left knee pain. I will order X-rays to evaluate for arthritis and rule out fracture. I recommend monitoring the patient's symptoms and adjusting the plan as needed.
