Chief Complaint
Right back pain, initially thought to be from hay handling, now suspected to be a kidney stone.

History of Present Illness
The patient reports onset of right back pain approximately one week ago while working in a barn, initially attributing it to hay handling. However, he experienced sudden onset of pain, nausea, and sweating, prompting him to seek medical attention. He has never experienced kidney stones before, although his father has a history of them. The pain radiated down to his groin, but has now localized to the right side. He has been straining his urine and has not seen any sediment in the urine. He reports continuing to experience occasional pain flares, but the overall pain has diminished.

Findings
Physical examination reveals tenderness on the right side of the abdomen. Vital signs are stable; blood pressure 130/80, heart rate 72, temperature 98.6°F. No fever detected. The patient reports no rebound tenderness or guarding. CT scan confirms a 0.5 cm stone located in the proximal right ureter, with no evidence of hydronephrosis.

Assessment
The patient presents with a confirmed kidney stone in the proximal right ureter. There is no evidence of acute kidney injury or obstruction.

Plan
I recommend pushing fluids to facilitate stone passage. Prescribing oxycodone 5 mg every 6-8 hours for pain, as needed, in addition to acetaminophen. Continue using the strainer provided. I'm ordering a BMP and urinalysis with urine culture to monitor kidney function and rule out infection. I will schedule a follow-up appointment in one to two weeks to assess stone passage. If the stone has not passed within that timeframe, we will discuss further treatment options, including lithotripsy.
