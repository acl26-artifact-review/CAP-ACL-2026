Chief Complaint
Evaluation of facial pain.

History of Present Illness
The patient reports experiencing stabbing, shooting pain in his right face for approximately two to three months. He describes the pain as being particularly intense in the cheekbone area and occasionally triggered by cold temperatures or sensory stimuli. He initially sought care from his family physician, who recommended a consultation with a neurosurgeon. The patient has not experienced any sensory loss or weakness and reports trying ibuprofen and tiger balm with minimal relief. He occasionally experiences headaches, but they are different from migraines he has experienced in the past. He denies numbness or tingling. He takes occasional tylenol for headaches.

Findings
Physical examination reveals facial symmetry and normal range of motion. Palpation of the right cheek reveals tenderness. Cotton tape testing reveals decreased sensation on the right side of the face. Neurological exam shows no facial weakness or abnormal cranial nerve function. An MRI revealed a small blood vessel abutting and potentially compressing the trigeminal nerve.

Assessment
The patient's symptoms and physical exam findings are consistent with trigeminal neuralgia. The MRI confirms compression of the trigeminal nerve.

Plan
I recommend starting with gabapentin to manage the pain. I will monitor for potential side effects, including dermatologic skin reactions. If the medication is not effective, we will discuss surgical options to decompress the nerve. Please schedule a follow-up appointment in one month to assess the response to treatment.
