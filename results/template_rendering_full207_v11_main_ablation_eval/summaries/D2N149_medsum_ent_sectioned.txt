Chief Complaint
Shortness of breath

History of Present Illness
The patient, Dennis, is a 57-year-old male presenting with a two-week history of shortness of breath. He reports waking up with shortness of breath and feeling short of breath upon returning home after an episode. He describes a tough time dealing with this, noting that he's been coughing up blood for approximately three weeks. He reports coughing up three to four cups of blood daily. He denies any bleeding elsewhere and reports no fever. He has been wearing a Dexcom and notes that his blood sugars spike up well over 200 after meals, and around 150 when he wakes up. His diabetes has been running a little high, and his hemoglobin A1c was around 7.7 previously. He has a history of asthma, diabetes, and high blood pressure, and reports taking norvasc some days due to low blood pressure. He had a knee problem some time ago but denies current swelling. He has not been to the hospital or emergency room for this issue. He drives and is able to self-transport to the emergency department.

Findings
Physical exam reveals no JVD, no neck swelling or carotid bruits. Lung exam shows some rales and rhonchi on the right side, with diminished breath sounds on the right. The heart exam reveals a two+ forceful ejection murmur, unchanged from previous examinations. The abdomen is soft. Lower extremity exam reveals one+ nonpitting edema on both ankles, with no calf tenderness. Oxygen saturation is 94% on pulse, and the pulse is regular. Blood pressure is approximately 120/70 mmHg.

Assessment
The patient presents with shortness of breath and productive cough with blood, likely secondary to an acute pulmonary process. His history of asthma, diabetes, and hypertension contribute to his current presentation.

Plan
I am ordering a chest x-ray and a CT scan of the chest to evaluate for a blood clot or other serious pathology. I will also send him to the emergency department for further evaluation. I will increase his metformin dose from 500mg twice daily to 750mg twice daily. I will follow up with him in three weeks to discuss his diabetes management and review the results of the imaging studies. I will hold the norvasc until we have better control of his symptoms.
