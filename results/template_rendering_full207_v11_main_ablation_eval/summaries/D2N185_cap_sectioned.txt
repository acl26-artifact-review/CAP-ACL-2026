Chief Complaint
Evaluation of abnormal renal ultrasound with an atrophic right kidney.

History of Present Illness
Ashley James, a 14-year-old female, presents with a history of an atrophic right kidney discovered through an ultrasound. The initial presentation occurred when she was 11 years old, complaining of stomach pain and reluctance to eat, which she described as rubbing her stomach. She initially thought it was an appendix. Her parents initially treated her for a stomach virus, providing fluids and sending her home. The next day, she was twice as bad as the first day. Then, they did an ultrasound and found that one kidney was bigger. Later, she was found to have cysts or something on her kidney and ovaries and maybe some other places. Her lymph nodes were swollen and they found some infection. She followed up with her primary care physician, but he was out of town on vacation. She then went to Jacksonville, where they confirmed the kidney was smaller. After that, she was seen by a geneticist, who found no clinically significant mutations. She has had several infections since she was four years old, and they didn't catch it in time. She has also had a history of posterior urethral strictures. She has also had end-stage renal disease and hypertension. She is currently taking pepcid and claritin. She is vomiting and experiences pain in her stomach and burning when she eats.

Findings
Physical exam reveals a healthy-appearing 14-year-old female, alert and oriented, sitting comfortably. Lung sounds are clear to auscultation bilaterally. Heart sounds are regular. Blood pressure is 120/80 mmHg. No edema is noted. The right kidney is smaller than the left.

Assessment
The patient presents with an atrophic right kidney and a history of recurrent infections and hypertension. She has end-stage renal disease and chronic kidney disease.

Plan
Recommend annual renal ultrasound and urinalysis. Continue Tylenol as needed for pain. Schedule physical therapy for shoulder strengthening and range of motion. Follow up in four weeks if symptoms persist. Refer to a nephrologist for further management.
