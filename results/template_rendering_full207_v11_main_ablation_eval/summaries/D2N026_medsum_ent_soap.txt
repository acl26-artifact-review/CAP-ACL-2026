S:
The patient reports intermittent left arm pain worsening at night for approximately two weeks. He describes waking at 2:00 AM with severe pain, followed by ice application providing relief. He notes the pain started after laying down and held his arm down, but this did not alleviate the pain. He reports some weakness in his hand, particularly when gripping things. He also reports a previous injury to his finger approximately one year ago, which does not work properly or works very rarely. The patient is currently taking eliquis and reports not being sure of the dosage. He states his wife helps him manage his medications. He denies taking any blood thinners.

O:
Physical exam reveals mild cervical restriction with limited extension, particularly to the side. Range of motion is symmetric. Tenderness to palpation is not present over the cervical spine. Reflexes are bilateral and are normal. Strength is one plus bilaterally in the biceps, triceps, and brachioradialis. There is no palpable edema or skin changes. Vital signs are within normal limits.

A:
The most likely diagnosis is cervical radiculopathy, likely secondary to disc degeneration. Peripheral neuropathy is also a consideration. The patient's symptoms are consistent with nerve root compression.

P:
I am ordering a cervical MRI to further evaluate the nerve roots and rule out other causes of the patient's symptoms. I am also recommending a left C7-T1 epidural to provide targeted anti-inflammatory medication to alleviate nerve root compression. The patient will continue taking gabapentin twice daily. I will follow up with the patient in one week to assess his response to treatment.
