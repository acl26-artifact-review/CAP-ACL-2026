S:
The patient reports a nonhealing foot ulcer present for approximately two months, recently becoming red and draining. He describes occasional throbbing pain, though not specific, and expresses concern about its healing. He reports taking medication but does not recall the name or frequency. He admits to not regularly checking his blood sugar levels. He states he had it from walking barefoot on the beach. The patient's last A1c was approximately ten. He reports no prior history of similar events.

O:
A one by two inch circular wound is present on the dorsal aspect of the lateral right foot, proximal to the fifth metatarsal head. The wound is red and draining. There is edema around the wound. No necrosis, odor, or bony exposure is observed. A physical exam reveals increased pressure but no distinct pain. An x-ray of the right foot showed no evidence of osteomyelitis. Vital signs are normal; temperature is within the normal range.

A:
The patient has a diabetic foot ulcer with signs of infection. The ulcer is likely related to his diabetes and potentially exacerbated by the beach exposure. Further investigation with an ABI is warranted to assess blood flow to the foot.

P:
I will order an ABI to assess blood flow to the foot. I will perform debridement of the ulcer. I will prescribe clindamycin 400mg twice daily for seven days to treat the infection. I will obtain a surgical shoe for the patient to wear to protect the ulcer. I will schedule a follow-up appointment in two weeks to assess healing and determine if further debridement is needed. I will counsel the patient on the importance of foot care and blood sugar control.
