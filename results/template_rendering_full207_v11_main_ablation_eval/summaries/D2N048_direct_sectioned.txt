Chief Complaint
Right foot pain.

History of Present Illness
The patient reports right foot pain that began approximately two days ago following a fall while playing tennis. He describes attempting to volley the ball and subsequently falling on his foot. He wrapped the foot with ace wraps and iced it last night. Ibuprofen provided some relief, but the effect wore off. He reports no prior injuries to this foot. He previously injured his left leg.

Findings
Physical exam reveals bruising of the plantar and dorsal aspects of the right foot. There is associated swelling in the midfoot. Palpation elicits tenderness in the midfoot. The piano key test of the first and second metatarsals is positive. Capillary refill is less than three seconds in the right foot. Dorsalis pedis pulse is strong. Neurovascular exam is intact. Vital signs are stable: blood pressure 125/70 mmHg, respiratory rate 18, temperature 97.1°F, and pulse 60 beats per minute. X-ray reveals subtle dorsal displacement of the base of the second metatarsal with a 3 mm separation of the first and second metatarsal bases and a bony fragment in the listerfranc joint space. Lung exam reveals mild stridor. Abdominal exam is unremarkable.

Assessment
Right foot pain, consistent with a Lisfranc fracture.

Plan
Recommend surgical intervention for the Lisfranc fracture to facilitate proper bone alignment and prevent complications such as poor ligament healing and potential for flatfoot development. Order an MRI to further assess the fracture and ligament injuries. Provide patient with information regarding the risks and benefits of surgery, including potential complications such as bleeding, nerve damage, and loss of sensation. Schedule a follow-up appointment in one week for cast removal and initial assessment of healing. Recommend a cast and crutches for six to eight weeks. Initiate physical therapy post-surgery to strengthen muscles and improve function. Provide patient with written instructions regarding cast care, crutch use, and activity restrictions. Schedule a follow-up appointment in four weeks if symptoms persist. Consider referral to a physical therapist for comprehensive rehabilitation.
