S:
The patient reports a foot wound that began approximately two to three weeks ago and is not healing. He reports no trauma to the foot and describes using Hoka shoes, noting they are comfortable and provide good stability, but occasionally experience stability issues. He states the wound looks worse now after wearing the shoes. The patient's last HbA1c was slightly above 8.0. He reports the wound sticks to his sock even when covered with a bandage. He denies any fever, chills, or drainage from the wound.

O:
Vital signs include a temperature of 98.1°F, blood pressure of 110/65 mmHg, heart rate of 72 beats per minute, and respiratory rate of 16 breaths per minute. Physical exam reveals a 1 x 2 inch circular wound on the dorsal aspect of the lateral right foot, proximal to the fifth metatarsal head. There is some yellow slough present with minimal granulation tissue. The surrounding skin is not erythematous or exhibiting signs of cellulitis. There is no evidence of fluid collection or necrosis, and no odor is appreciated. B/P pulses are palpable bilaterally. Doppler studies reveal adequate blood flow. An x-ray of the right foot shows no evidence of osteomyelitis.

A:
The patient has a diabetic foot ulcer. The clinician suspects potential vascular compromise contributing to the wound's delayed healing. A vascular consultation may be warranted.

P:
The clinician orders an ankle brachial index (ABI) to assess blood flow to the foot. A debridement will be performed in the office. A collagenase ointment will be prescribed for daily application to the wound, covered with a dry sterile dressing. A surgical shoe will be worn to provide pressure relief. The patient will be seen again in two weeks for follow-up. If the ABI indicates suboptimal blood supply, a referral to a vascular specialist will be arranged. A diabetic consult will be scheduled to discuss dietary modifications and potential HbA1c management.
