S:
The patient reports a six-month history of knee pain, initially without a known cause. He describes the pain as a deep, achy sensation, particularly with sitting and going up and down stairs. The pain is exacerbated by prolonged sitting and relieved somewhat by rest. He has tried ibuprofen, aleve, and tylenol with limited relief. He denies any prior knee injuries or fractures. He reports running five to six miles daily, but the knee pain has reduced his running to half a mile. He also enjoys cycling, which he finds less problematic.

O:
Blood pressure is 120/70 mmHg. Heart rate is 60 bpm, regular. Respiratory rate is 14 breaths per minute. Gait is normal. Muscle strength is good, with abduction of the legs bilaterally rated as 3/5. Patellar grind test is positive. Lachman's test, anterior and posterior drawer test, and McMurray test are all negative. X-rays of both knees show no fractures or osteoarthritis. No redness, swelling, or warmth is noted. Neurological exam is normal.

A:
Based on the patient's history and examination, patellofemoral pain syndrome is the most likely diagnosis. The absence of fractures or osteoarthritis on the X-rays supports this assessment. The positive patellar grind test and reported pain with squatting further corroborate this diagnosis.

P:
Recommend physical therapy to address lower extremity strength and correct running mechanics. Continue ibuprofen or aleve as needed for pain relief. Schedule a follow-up appointment in four weeks to assess progress and adjust treatment plan as needed. Advise the patient to gradually increase activity levels while monitoring for pain flare-ups.
