Chief Complaint
Evaluation of a new right eye twitch.

History of Present Illness
Karen Nelson, a 3-year-old female, presents with a report of a new right eye twitch that her father noticed approximately two weeks ago. The patient reports feeling her face sometimes and occasionally experiences an urge to move her face, which then resolves. She reports that the twitch varies in frequency, sometimes occurring several times per hour, and other days it is not noticeable until late afternoon. The patient reports that she occasionally experiences the urge to blink her eye and feels relief afterwards. She denies any associated pain. She reports that she likes playing soccer and enjoys playing with her friends, but occasionally experiences rough play with a girl named Isabella who kicks her and pulls her hair. After soccer, she sometimes experiences MCD's and finds it enjoyable. She has no prior history of seizures or Tourette's syndrome and reports no family history of either. She has been eating well and playing with her friends and is otherwise acting normally. She has not experienced any fever, cough, or headache. She denies any problems with sleep. She has not noticed any other symptoms.

Findings
Physical exam reveals the patient to be awake, alert, and oriented to three. Gait is steady. Cranial nerves are intact. Reflexes are two plus and symmetric at the biceps, triceps, knees, and ankles. Neurological exam shows no pronator drift. The patient's right eye twitch was observed during the examination. The patient was able to follow her finger, reach out her arms, and keep her eyes closed as instructed. There was no difference in sensation between the two sides of her face.

Assessment
Karen Nelson presents with a transient tic, likely a right eye twitch. The tic is not associated with any neurological abnormalities or other concerning symptoms. Given the transient nature of the tic and the patient's overall well-being, further investigation is not immediately indicated.

Plan
I do not recommend any treatment at this time. I will order radiographs of the right eye to rule out any structural abnormalities. I will recommend physical therapy to help manage the tic and provide exercises to improve muscle strength and coordination. I will schedule a follow-up appointment in four weeks to assess the patient's progress and adjust the treatment plan as needed. I will counsel the patient's father on strategies to minimize attention to the tic and encourage him to provide a supportive and encouraging environment.
