Chief Complaint
Palpitations and recent emergency room visit for high blood pressure.

History of Present Illness
The patient reports a longstanding history of hypertension and palpitations, with palpitations returning recently. He describes experiencing palpitations in March, coinciding with a few cups of coffee and no breakfast. He attributes these episodes to a panic attack. He has been taking a combination medication (40-25) for approximately one month, prescribed by a new primary care physician. He reports his father and other family members have a history of high blood pressure. He is currently taking amlodipine 10 milligrams daily. He checks his blood pressure at home every morning, noting readings between 146 and 155 mmHg. He is attempting to reduce his salt intake. The patient reports feeling that his blood pressure is getting better, but is still not down to the desired level. He has a potassium level of 4.0 from one week ago. He is concerned about potential side effects of new medications.

Findings
Physical exam reveals normal heart and lung sounds. Examination of the ankles and feet reveals no signs of edema. Blood pressure readings at home range from 146 to 155 mmHg. The patient reports no current symptoms beyond palpitations.

Assessment
The patient presents with resistant hypertension, despite being on maximal medication doses. Further investigation is warranted to determine the underlying cause.

Plan
Prescribe spironolactone to help manage aldosterone levels and lower blood pressure. Order a metabolic panel and blood pressure check in two weeks to monitor electrolyte levels. Counsel the patient on the importance of dietary sodium reduction and regular exercise, recommending the DASH eating plan and the American Heart Association website for further information. Schedule a follow-up appointment in two weeks to assess response to treatment and monitor electrolytes. If symptoms persist, consider referral to a resistant hypertension clinic for further evaluation and potential renal denervation.
