S:
The patient reports a two to three week history of a non-healing wound on his right foot. He states he experienced a blister that worsened, and has been applying a band-aid. He denies fever, chills, or drainage from the wound. He reports the wound does not hurt and occasionally sticks to his sock when it's removed. He reports his diabetes control is fair, with a recent HbA1c of approximately 8.0%. He states he is wearing Hoka shoes, which he finds comfortable and stable, and purchased them about two years ago from REI. He notes occasional stability issues with those shoes.

O:
The physical exam reveals a 1 x 2 inch circular wound on the dorsal aspect of the lateral right foot, approximately proximal to the fifth to fifth MTPI. There is yellow slough present with minimal granulation tissue. There is no surrounding erythema or cellulitis, no fluid collection, no necrosis, and no odor. B/P is 110/65. Pulses are palpable bilaterally. Right foot x-ray shows no evidence of osteomyelitis. Temperature is 98.1°F. Heart rate is 72. Respirations are 16.

A:
The primary problem is a diabetic foot ulcer. The patient's right foot presents with a wound that is not healing appropriately. There is no evidence of osteomyelitis. The patient's diabetes is currently under control, but could benefit from further optimization.

P:
I am ordering an ankle brachial index (ABI) to assess blood flow to the foot. I will perform a debridement in the office. I am prescribing collagenase ointment to be applied daily to the wound, covered with a dry sterile dressing. I will order a surgical shoe to apply pressure to the area. I am not prescribing antibiotics at this time. I will schedule a follow-up appointment in two weeks. If the ABI indicates suboptimal blood supply, I will consider a referral to a vascular specialist. I will arrange a diabetic consult to discuss dietary modifications and potential HbA1c optimization.
