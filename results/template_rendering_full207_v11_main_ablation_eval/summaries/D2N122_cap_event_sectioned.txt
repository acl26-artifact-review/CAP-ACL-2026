Chief Complaint
Persistent cough and a newly discovered lung nodule.

History of Present Illness
The patient reports a persistent cough for approximately three months, initially associated with a recent illness and exacerbated by loading bills. He describes the cough as intermittent and notes it worsened after a cold. He reports a history of rheumatoid arthritis managed with methotrexate (7.5 mg weekly) and reports feeling generally well with his RA, though experiencing occasional hand swelling. He quit smoking seven years ago and reports feeling better since. He denies any recent pain or shortness of breath. The patient is notably anxious about the lung nodule discovered during the chest x-ray.

Findings
Physical exam reveals blood pressure 120/76, heart rate 70, respiration rate 16, and oxygen saturation 98% on room air. The patient is afebrile. Lung auscultation reveals clear and equal bilateral lung sounds. Examination reveals mild swelling to the knuckles on the third digit of each hand, consistent with rheumatoid arthritis. Pulses are intact, and capillary refill is brisk. Chest CT demonstrates a solitary 2 cm nodule in the right upper lobe, appearing smooth and without evidence of emphysema.

Assessment
The patient presents with a persistent cough and a newly identified solitary lung nodule. Clinical suspicion is raised for a potential malignancy, given the smoking history and the presence of the nodule. Rheumatoid arthritis remains under control with current medication regimen.

Plan
Order pulmonary function testing to assess lung function. Schedule a PET-CT scan to evaluate the nodule for metabolic activity. Recommend video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery (VATS) to remove the nodule, with general anesthesia and a chest tube for one night. Continue methotrexate as prescribed by the rheumatologist. Schedule a follow-up appointment in four weeks to assess progress and discuss further management options.
