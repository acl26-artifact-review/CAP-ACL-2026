Chief Complaint
Hematuria related to a suspected kidney stone.

History of Present Illness
The patient reports a history of hematuria, initially noted six weeks ago, accompanied by episodes of bright red urine resembling 'cherry kool-aid.' He also experienced significant pain, rated as an eight out of ten, during those episodes. Last week, he passed two small stones. Following the stone passages, his pain has significantly decreased. He is currently taking Alavert 10mg daily and reports no drug allergies. He admits to drinking primarily Mountain Dew and expresses a desire to increase his water intake.

Findings
Physical exam reveals no current flank pain or tenderness to palpation. The vaginal exam was unremarkable. Renal ultrasound demonstrated a 6mm left renal stone, non-obstructing, within a 10cm x 5cm right kidney and a 10.8cm x 5cm left kidney with a 6mm stone. Pre-void volume was 533ml and post-void volume was 0ml. Urine dipstick was negative for leukocytes, nitrates, protein, ketone, bilirubin, and glucose. Color was yellow and clear, with urobilinogen 0.2 and a specific gravity of 1.020. The trace amount of blood in the urine was consistent with the previously identified kidney stone.

Assessment
The patient presents with hematuria secondary to a confirmed left renal stone. There is no evidence of obstruction or significant pain at this time.

Plan
I recommend continued monitoring of the left renal stone. We should focus on increasing daily fluid intake to approximately 125 ounces, based on a 250-pound weight, and avoiding sugary drinks like Mountain Dew. I'm providing a handout detailing dietary recommendations to limit sodium oxalate and animal protein. Follow up in one year unless the patient experiences recurrent bleeding or pain. If severe pain or significant bleeding occurs, the patient should seek immediate emergency room care.
