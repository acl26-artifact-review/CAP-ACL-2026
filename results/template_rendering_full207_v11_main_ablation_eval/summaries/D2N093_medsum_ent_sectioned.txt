Chief Complaint
Shortness of breath

History of Present Illness
The patient reports shortness of breath, which has been present for approximately 10 days. He attributes this to swelling and lethargy, potentially related to dietary indiscretions, such as eating salty foods and attending house parties. He notes some swelling in his legs and reports feeling stiff upon waking, with some difficulty adjusting upon standing. He has experienced one episode of acute shortness of breath approximately a week ago, but it has not recurred. He is currently managing his type 1 diabetes with an insulin pump and reports that his blood sugars have been generally stable during the working week, although he admits to occasionally forgetting to monitor them on weekends. He has been taking omeprazole for reflux and has made lifestyle modifications, reducing his red bull and caffeine intake. He reports no issues with depression and denies any suicidal ideation. He has been utilizing natural solutions for depression, such as outdoor stretches, but has not resumed them due to the change in weather. He is open to exploring medication options for depression.

Findings
Physical exam reveals no jugular venous distension or carotid bruits. The heart exam reveals a mild, 3/6 systolic ejection murmur. Lung exam reveals bilateral crackles at the bases. Lower extremity exam reveals 1+ pitting edema. Vital signs are stable, with oxygen saturation within normal limits at rest. Chest x-ray demonstrates no evidence of airspace disease. Lab results show no elevated white blood cell count, suggesting no acute infection.

Assessment
The patient is experiencing an acute heart failure exacerbation, likely secondary to fluid retention and underlying congestive heart failure. His shortness of breath, edema, and crackles support this diagnosis.

Plan
I am ordering lasix, 40 milligrams daily, and will instruct the patient to monitor his weight daily and to call if his shortness of breath worsens or if he experiences any significant weight gain. I will also order a hemoglobin A1C test in one month to assess his diabetes control. I will refer the patient to psychiatry for further evaluation and management of his depression. Continue omeprazole 20 milligrams daily. Advise the patient to call immediately if he experiences any worsening of his symptoms or if he is unable to tolerate oral medications.
