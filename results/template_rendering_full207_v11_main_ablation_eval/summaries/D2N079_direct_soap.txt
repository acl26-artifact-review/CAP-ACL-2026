S:
The patient reports left shoulder pain for approximately three weeks, initially unsure of the inciting event, attributing it to basement renovation work. He reports difficulty with reaching and lifting, particularly overhead. He has been taking two extra-strength Tylenol every six to eight hours, which provides some relief but not complete resolution. He denies numbness or tingling in his arms or hands. He reports no prior shoulder pain episodes easily managed with Tylenol.

O:
Physical exam reveals limited active and passive range of motion in the left shoulder, with reported pain. Tenderness is noted to the greater tuberosity of the humerus. Hand grips are good. Capillary refill is less than three seconds in the left arm. Sensation to light touch is intact. Shoulder x-ray shows no fractures.

A:
The patient's symptoms are most likely due to rotator cuff tendinopathy. A rotator cuff injury is suspected based on the patient's reported pain and functional limitations.

P:
Order an MRI of the left shoulder to evaluate for rotator cuff pathology. Refer the patient to physical therapy for approximately six to eight weeks. Continue Tylenol as needed for pain relief. Consider a steroid injection if symptoms do not improve after six to eight weeks of physical therapy. Schedule a follow-up visit in four weeks to assess progress.
