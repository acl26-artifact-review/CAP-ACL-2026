S:
The patient reports knee pain for approximately six months, initially with no identified trauma. He describes a deep, aching pain behind his kneecaps and occasional crepitus. He reports pain worsening with activities such as rising from a seated position, watching television, and climbing stairs. He states that rest provides some relief. He has tried ice and ace bandages and occasional ibuprofen or aleve. He reports running five to six miles daily but now can only manage half a mile before experiencing leg pain. He enjoys running outdoors, particularly on Boston marathon routes. He denies swelling or redness in his knees and has no prior knee injuries, except for a sixth-grade arm fracture. He reports no current medications beyond occasional ibuprofen. He states he tries to be active, enjoying running five to six miles daily, but now can only manage half a mile before experiencing leg pain.

O:
Vital signs are within normal limits. Heart exam reveals regular rate and rhythm with no murmurs. Gait is normal. Strength is 3/5 for abduction bilaterally. Knee exam reveals no erythema, effusion, or warmth. Palpation of the retinacular retinaculum is tender. Patellar grind test is positive. Lachman's tests are negative. McMurray's tests are negative. X-ray of both knees shows no fractures or osteoarthritis. Neurological exam reveals symmetrical patella and Achilles reflexes.

A:
The patient's symptoms are consistent with patellofemoral pain syndrome. The examination findings support this diagnosis, with tenderness to palpation and a positive patellar grind test. There is no evidence of acute injury or structural abnormalities.

P:
Recommend physical therapy to address lower extremity strength, mobility, and running mechanics. Advise continued use of ibuprofen or aleve as needed for pain relief. Schedule a follow-up appointment in four weeks to assess progress and adjust treatment plan. Encourage the patient to take it easy from running for now, but continue to assess progress with each visit.
