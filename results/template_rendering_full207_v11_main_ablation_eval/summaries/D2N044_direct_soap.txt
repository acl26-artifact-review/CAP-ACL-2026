S:
The patient reports knee pain for approximately six months, initially without a known inciting event. He describes the pain as a deep, achy sensation located behind the kneecaps. The pain is exacerbated by sitting and prolonged periods of inactivity, as well as by standing and walking, particularly up and down stairs. He reports that the pain improves with rest. He has been taking ibuprofen and aleve intermittently to manage the pain. He denies any prior knee injuries or fractures. He works from home and his office is located downstairs. He enjoys working from home and his commute, and he loves biking. He is a big runner and has been running five to six miles a day, but with the knee pain he has been barely able to do half a mile. He has noticed no redness or swelling in his knees. He has not noticed any neurological deficits.

O:
Blood pressure is 120/70 mmHg. Heart rate is 60 bpm. Respiratory rate is 14 breaths per minute. Gait is normal. Muscle strength is good, with abduction being 3/5 bilaterally. Lachman, anterior and posterior drawer, and McMurray tests are negative bilaterally. Patellar and Achilles reflexes are symmetrical. X-rays of both knees show no fractures or osteoarthritis. No effusion is appreciated. Palpation reveals tenderness at the patella. The patellar grind test is positive upon standing.

A:
The patient's symptoms are consistent with patellofemoral pain syndrome. The MRI prior to presentation supports this diagnosis. The lack of fracture or osteoarthritis on the x-rays further supports this diagnosis.

P:
Recommend physical therapy to address lower extremity strength and correct running mechanics. Continue ibuprofen or aleve as needed for pain relief. Schedule a follow-up appointment in four weeks to assess progress and adjust treatment plan as needed. Advise the patient to take it easy and cross pads on the bike trail some day.
