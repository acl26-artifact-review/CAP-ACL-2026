S:
The patient reports a history of hypertension since childhood and reports that his blood pressure was getting better before starting a new medication a month ago. He states he had a few cups of coffee and no breakfast on the day he presented to the emergency room in March with high blood pressure and palpitations. He denies feeling palpitations since. He reports that his potassium level was 4.0 one week ago. He is trying to back off of his salt intake and is doing good with it, not eating too much. He is trying to walk on the treadmill when he can. He is currently taking benicar hct 10mg daily and amlodipine 10mg daily. He reports that he is checking his blood pressure every morning when he gets up and it has been running at about 146 to 155 lately. He is doing okay with his medications and is willing to try spironolactone.

O:
Physical exam reveals heart and lungs to be clear to auscultation. Peripheral pulses are strong and equal bilaterally. Lower extremity exam reveals no edema. Blood pressure measured 146/90 mmHg. Potassium 4.0.

A:
The patient presents with resistant hypertension despite being on maximal doses of multiple medications. Further investigation is warranted to determine the underlying cause of his hypertension. Spironolactone is being considered as a potential treatment option.

P:
Order spironolactone 25mg daily. Schedule a follow-up appointment in two weeks to monitor blood pressure and electrolytes. Provide the patient with information on the DASH diet and the American Heart Association website for further education on managing his blood pressure. Advise patient to monitor for potential side effects of spironolactone, including dizziness, gynecomastia, and electrolyte imbalances. Consider referral to a resistant hypertension clinic if symptoms persist despite treatment.
