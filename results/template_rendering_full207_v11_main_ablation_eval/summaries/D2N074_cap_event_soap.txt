S:
The patient reports worsening headaches over the past few months, initially three out of 10 in severity, now approximately six out of 10. He describes the pain as a dull, nagging ache located behind his eyes, lasting for several hours. He also reports recent bumping into door frames and a perceived decrease in peripheral vision, noticed only after the eye exam. He denies any fever, rash, neck stiffness, numbness, tingling, or passing out. He has not taken any medications for the headaches and reports no alcohol, tobacco, or recreational drug use.

O:
Neurological examination reveals bitemporal hemianopia. Visual acuity and eye movements are within normal limits. Blood pressure is mildly low. The MRI reveals a pituitary lesion with elevation and compression of the optic chiasm, with ventricles of normal size.

A:
The patient has a pituitary lesion, likely a benign pituitary adenoma, causing optic chiasm compression and bitemporal hemianopia.

P:
Refer the patient to an ophthalmologist for a comprehensive visual field evaluation prior to surgery. Schedule a consultation with a neurosurgeon to discuss surgical options for decompression of the pituitary lesion. Arrange for a transsphenoidal approach for surgical intervention.
