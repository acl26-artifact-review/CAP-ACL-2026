Chief Complaint
Abdominal pain

History of Present Illness
Karen is a 34-year-old female presenting with abdominal pain that started ten days ago with mild discomfort in her right upper quadrant, which has been getting worse over the last couple of days, especially. The pain is generally around a four or five on a scale, but sometimes gets up to a seven or eight. It seems to be worse after a meal, and she notes some nausea and heartburn, but no vomiting. She reports that the pain radiates a little to the left, and is tender to the touch in the right upper quadrant. She has not noticed any changes in her bowel habits. Karen reports chills but no fever. She has been taking excedrin migraine occasionally for breakthrough migraines and sumatriptan as needed. She has not been checking her blood pressures daily. She recently traveled to Wisconsin but has no other unusual exposures.

Findings
Examination reveals right upper quadrant tenderness, an equivocal Murphy's sign, and a grade 3 systolic ejection murmur. Her blood pressure is elevated at 128/78. She reports chills but no fever.

Assessment
The doctor suspects an infection in the gallbladder may be contributing to the abdominal pain. Gallstones are considered a possible cause of abdominal pain.

Plan
The doctor ordered a complete blood count, a refill of lisinopril, and scheduled a follow-up. The doctor prescribed sumatriptan as needed for breakthrough migraine pain. The patient reports right upper quadrant tenderness.
