Chief Complaint
Right flank pain radiating to the groin, concerning for a possible kidney stone.

History of Present Illness
The patient reports experiencing right flank pain for the past two weeks, which radiates down to his groin. The pain is intermittent and depends on fluid intake and activity levels, but is consistently present. He describes the pain as 'under control' but with bouts where it becomes more intense. He reports no urinary symptoms such as dysuria or hematuria, although he did experience a brief episode of testicular pain, which subsided. He has taken ibuprofen for pain relief, which provides some comfort. He has a history of passing kidney stones previously, both independently and with surgical intervention. He notes that family members have also experienced similar issues. He denies nausea, vomiting, chills, or fever. He reports being a 'horrible active' by his own admission.

Findings
Physical examination reveals blood pressure of 138/82 mmHg, which is slightly elevated due to pain. Palpation of the abdomen reveals tenderness in the right flank, consistent with the reported pain. There is no reported tenderness to palpation of the abdomen. The patient reports no other significant findings on physical exam.

Assessment
The patient presents with a likely kidney stone, based on the reported symptoms and physical exam findings. The CT scan confirms a 0.5 cm stone located on the proximal right ureter, without evidence of obstruction or high-flow nephrosis.

Plan
I will continue the patient on ibuprofen for pain management. I am prescribing tramadol 50mg for breakthrough pain. I will order a BMP, urinalysis, and urine culture to rule out infection. I will schedule a follow-up appointment in one to two weeks to assess progress and discuss further treatment options, including possible lithotripsy if needed. I will also advise the patient to continue using the strainer when urinating to attempt to capture the stone. Please have the patient follow up with me in two weeks.
