Chief Complaint
Patient reports an ulcer on his right foot that has been present for approximately six to eight weeks.

History of Present Illness
The patient reports that the ulcer on his right foot has been present for approximately six to eight weeks. He states that it was likely caused by stepping on a nail while working in the backyard several weeks ago. He reports that his blood sugar levels have been running higher than normal, and he occasionally experiences mild foot pain, particularly when it's colder outside. He denies any fever or chills. He reports occasional headaches, often attributed to stress. The patient reports occasional numbness in his feet, particularly in colder weather. He quit smoking approximately four years ago after smoking for over 20 years. He currently takes aspirin daily as prescribed.

Findings
Physical exam reveals a two by three centimeter ulcerated lesion on the right lateral foot near the fifth metatarsophalangeal joint. There is no associated cellulitis. The wound demonstrates granulation tissue and some purulent discharge. Vital signs are as follows: blood pressure 127/78 mmHg, heart rate 64 bpm, temperature 98.6°F. Dorsalis pedis and posterior tibial pulses are palpable. Jugular venous distension and carotid bruits are absent. Lung sounds are clear bilaterally. The heart murmur is a two out of six systolic ejection murmur heard at the left base.

Assessment
The patient presents with a non-healing ulcer on the right foot, likely secondary to underlying diabetes and vascular insufficiency. His history of smoking and current blood sugar control contribute to the risk of complications.

Plan
I am ordering an arterial ultrasound to assess vascular supply to the foot. I am also requesting a podiatry consultation to manage the ulcer and provide wound care. The patient should continue with aspirin as prescribed. I will discuss the ultrasound results and podiatry recommendations with the patient and coordinate further management as needed. I will schedule a follow-up appointment in four weeks to assess progress.
