S:
The patient reports a history of kidney stones, with this episode being the third occurrence. He describes significant pain, starting three days ago, and reports continued pain with some radiation to his lower back. The pain is constant and makes it difficult to find comfortable positions. He reports nausea and some chills. He has been taking Tylenol and ibuprofen to alleviate the pain, but finds them ineffective. He reports seeing some blood in his urine this morning. He has not passed the stone yet. He reports difficulty taking his blood pressures regularly, with readings ranging from one thirty to one forty for the top number and seventy to eighty for the bottom number. He has been trying to curb his french fry habit, but finds it difficult. He reports having taken his metformin daily.

O:
The patient's blood pressure was slightly elevated at 138/82 mmHg. The patient's heart rate was 82 bpm and regular. Physical exam reveals tenderness to palpation in the right flank. A grade 2 systolic ejection murmur is auscultated. Lung sounds are clear bilaterally. The patient's abdomen is tender to palpation. Neurological exam is unremarkable.

A:
The patient presents with a likely kidney stone, experiencing significant pain and nausea. His history of recurrent kidney stones warrants further investigation. His blood pressure is slightly elevated, likely due to the pain. He is at risk for complications related to his kidney stone and hypertension.

P:
Rescribe oxycodone 5 mg every 6-8 hours as needed for pain. Continue Tylenol as needed for breakthrough pain. Provide a strainer for urine to monitor stone passage. Refer to urology for further evaluation and management of recurrent kidney stones. Provide a referral to a registered dietitian for assistance with dietary modifications to reduce sodium intake. Schedule a follow-up appointment in 2 weeks to assess progress and adjust management plan.
