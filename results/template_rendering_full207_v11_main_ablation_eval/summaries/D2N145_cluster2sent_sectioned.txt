Chief Complaint
Perioral dermatitis and rosacea.

History of Present Illness
The patient reports experiencing perioral dermatitis and rosacea, initially prompted by concerns about breastfeeding and subsequent use of doxycycline. She discontinued breastfeeding and requested doxycycline at that time. She was previously using Elidel, but became worried about it after reading about it and opted to try doxycycline instead. She is using the sulfacetamide wash and metro cream, but not the cetaphil cleanser or Neutrogena wipes. She is Fitzpatrick skin type IV, meaning she tans rather than burns. There are faint erythematous papules on her bilateral medial cheeks. She reports that doxycycline can cause upset stomach or heartburn. She is currently taking doxycycline twice a day, but the provider discussed reducing it to once a day if the rash improves. She mentioned that the eucrisa recommended by her notes is expensive, and she could try the ointment form instead. She applies the tacrolimus ointment to the rash area twice daily, noting a tingly or needle-like sensation initially. She wants to continue using the vanicream cleanser and petroleum jelly if needed. She is instructed to gently wash her eyelids once a day with vanicream and to use Bruder mask as needed. The patient reports that she will apply the rash on her face twice daily until resolved.

Findings
The patient reports sunburn even without sun exposure when using the tacrolimus ointment. Correct, the medication is not a steroid. It is a calcineurin inhibitor, similar to a steroid in that it calms inflammation, but it does not cause thinning of the skin or other rashes associated with steroids. The patient is using the doxycycline twice a day.

Assessment
The patient presents with perioral dermatitis and rosacea, likely exacerbated by prior immunosuppression.

Plan
The provider instructed the patient to continue using the vanicream cleanser, metro cream, and to increase the use of doxycycline to twice a day. The provider recommended stopping the doxycycline after one week if the rash improves. If the rash flares again, the patient can try the tacrolimus ointment for a couple of days. The provider instructed the patient to avoid any anti-aging or plant-based products. The provider cancelled the Elidel cream and instructed the patient to continue with the doxycycline until the rash resolves, then discontinue the tacrolimus ointment. The provider will monitor the patient's response to treatment and adjust the plan as needed.
