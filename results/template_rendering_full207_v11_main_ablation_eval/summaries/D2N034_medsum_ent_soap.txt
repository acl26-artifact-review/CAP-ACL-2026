S:
The patient reports left shoulder pain for the last three weeks, which is not improving. He describes pain as constant and exacerbated by movement, particularly reaching or lifting. He reports icing the initial injury but has not used icing recently. He denies numbness or tingling in the arm or fingers. He has not taken any medication for the pain.

O:
Physical exam reveals limited active and passive range of motion in the left shoulder with tenderness to palpation over the greater tuberosity of the humerus. Capillary refill is less than three seconds in the left hand. Neurological exam is intact with no sensory deficits. X-ray of the shoulder is normal, showing no fractures or bony abnormalities.

A:
The patient's symptoms are most likely due to rotator cuff tendinopathy. An MRI of the left shoulder is ordered to rule out other causes and assess the extent of the injury.

P:
I am referring the patient to physical therapy for approximately six to eight weeks to strengthen the shoulder muscles. Continue taking Tylenol as needed for pain relief. If symptoms do not improve, a steroid injection may be considered. Follow up in four weeks to assess progress.
